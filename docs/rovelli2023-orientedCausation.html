(p. 9) At the light of this insightful consideration, causality is better understood, I believe, not as a simple physical issue, and even less as a metaphysical one, but rather in terms of the perspective and interest of deliberating human agents.

** (p. 4) The way this works can be briefly synthetized as follows. Assume we have a number of variables A, B, C, D, … that are partially ordered in time, and we know—experimentally or theoretically—that the values that these variables can take are correlated. Assume we know these correlations. Then we say that the variable B is causally related to a variable C that follows it in time if the following happens. If we disregard the correlations between B and earlier variables, that is, we allow these correlations to violated, and intervene setting the variable of B at different values, then the (known) correlations in the future of B imply that differences in the value of B affect the value of C.

a. intervention

It is, to put it mildly, not clear if this fits the model outlined.

Now it is the intervention that causes an effect and not a state/variable (B in the description) that was part of the system/(the non-interventioned sequence of variables/systems states). (Is a variable a thing that can be varied or a symbol that can represent various things?) There appears to be confusion between a variable, a value of a variable and (a dimension of) states of the system. Unclear if the confusion is mine or the authors.

Still I think I am happy with the spirit if not the details. If we regard something as an external agent acting freely then an action of that free agent - the intervention - causes the changes from how a system would have evolved if that intervention had not occurred. But this suggestion does not amount to giving a direction of time.

b. time orientation

This is the meatiest part of the paper

(p. 9) the assumption that breaks time reversal invariance is the assumption that intervention of the external agent affects the future, not the past. It is important to notice that this is assumed in the definition of causation; intervention is assumed to break past correlations, while future ones are preserved and determine the effect

Perhaps most consideration is given to the example of a stone falling into a pond. (Or at least it is supposed to be by consideration of this example that we see what breaks the time reversal symmetry.) But the author does not quite succeed in lining up this case with the account of causation given in ** above.

1st problem: example doesn't fit model

To begin with he considers the stone as the intervention (or perhaps it is the stone's falling into the pond - lets not quibble about that). But in ** the causal link was supposed to exist between B and C, and the intervention was altering the value of variable B. But B was part of the non-interventionist sequence of events, wheras in the stone example, the stone(/its falling) is the both the intervention and the cause. Does he want to say that the pond being flat and smooth at B causes the pond to be flat and smooth at time C? Doesn't seem like it.

To make the example fit the model perhaps we can consider the stone as part of the system and we intervene to alter the stones trajectory. So B is the stone falling with a certain trajectory/velocity and if we intervene to alter B then the pattern of waves (which would be C and D in the ** pattern) are, according to physical laws/correlations altered.

when is t = 0 and what is held fixed ?

A falling is not something that occurs at a temporal instant. Fallings as those have enjoyed them can attest, take time. At the beginning of a fall one tends to be up and at the end of falling one tends to be down.

This is important because when the author gives us the following choice:

(a) if the history of the pond had been the same as in the history with the falling stone for all times t < 0,
or
(b) if the history of the pond had been the same as in the history with the falling stone for all times t > 0.

and apparently we don't choose (b) because:

In [b] we have to find a past history h of the pond (never hit by anything) such that from time t = 0 onward there are spherical outgoing waves from a certain location. This history certainly exists (the water of the pond might have moved in all sort of ways in the past), but it looks strangely implausible to us.

But this isn't right. Why do we need to fix never hit by anything in the past history of the pond. When we choose option (a) we explicitly don't stipulate that we must have a future history of the pond no expanding waves. In the normal case what happens for t > 0 is determined by each preceeding moment. So too in the reversed case what happens for t < 0 is determined by each subsequent (i.e. t larger) moment. We don't get to stipulate that the pond is not hit/exited by anything.

Look at the whole thing in reverse: Now the intervention is the pattern of concentric wave patterns one sends converging onto a rock that is lying on the bottom of the pond. Or perhaps the minor jiggles one makes to the ponds edges to generate*time reversed those waves. If one intervenes and varies those converging waves then the rock leaves by various different trajectories. t = 0 is when those waves are emitted/absorbed by the pond edges.

If on the other hand we stick to the original scheme then t = 0 is when the rock enters stage left, or maybe when it strikes the water, or maybe when it hits the bottom of the pond. Lets say its that last. Say we intervene at t = 0 (when rock hits bottom) say by sticking our finger on the rock at the moment it is struck by those converging waves, then what happens is that instead of becoming a calm and flat pond with a rock exiting stage left, it is now a confused (largely expanding but affected by the exact shape of the rock) set of waves on a rock containing pond. The intervention of holding the rock in place causes the confused waves rebounding across the pond (and the rock to stay in place).

In either case it seems perfectly coherent and compatible with the interventionist model of causation to say that the pattern of waves caused to rock to be spat out of the pond OR to say that holding the rock in place at t = 0 caused confused waves and the rock to stay in place. Indeed if we saw things happening in that reversed order, it seems to me that that is exactly what we would say.

TAKE HOME: Thus far we have found no reason to prefer saying that the rock falling in caused the concentric waves rather than saying that the concentric waves caused the rock to (anti-)fall.

thermodynamics to the rescue

At this point the author invokes thermodynamics to explain why we prefer option (a) over option (b).

The key point is that this argument is not about the mechanical laws of physics: These are compatible with the history h. It is something else that makes h strange. What is it? It is thermodynamics: we know that systems equilibrate in time. Thermodynamics has more ingredients than elementary physical laws: a macroscopic description of the world and the observation that entropy was low in the past.

where history h is the history of option (b) - either the history in which there are subsequently diverging waves but no rock (if one tries to follow authors suggestion) or the reversed future of converging*time reversed waves which cause the rock to be spat out of the pond

This is so unsatisfying that if I didn't quote it in full a reader might think I am being uncharitable:

(p. 6) In Lewis’s terminology, the world without intervention wo can be compared with a world with intervention wp having the same past as wo, or a world with intervention wf having the same future as wo . Both wp and wf satisfy the dynamical laws, but wp satisfies also the second law of thermodynamics (and its generalizations) while wf does not. Hence, wp is a see world far closer to ours (again in Lewis language) than wf. This is why we talk about wp as a reasonable possibility and not so about wf. And this is therefore why we consider the effect to be in the future of the cause.

This account makes clear why the effect of the intervention is in the future of the intervention and not in its past: Because it is in the past that the overall system was away from equilibrium, the stone had more energy than what equipartition of energy indicates. Because of this past low entropy, the thermodynamical evolution of the system is time- oriented, as for all thermodynamical systems with past low entropy.

This amounts to saying: we know that the past has lower entropy than the future and so we will only consider alternatives in which entropy is increasing.

(p. 7) Causation is therefore a macroscopic thermodynamic phenomenon where the total entropy is raised by an intervention, and the effect is the trace left on the system by the intervention.

Just to make clear how unsatisfactory this is - imagine a physicist who carefully sets up a mechanical pond edge jiggler and carefully develops it and tests it to send a band of carefully directed waves to converge upon and spit out a specific rock on the bottom of a pond.

Now she lets the system sit and cool for a while. Considered in isolation during this cool off period the entropy of the system is unchanging. (Ok maybe some corrosion of the gears or evaporation/degradation of the lubrication.) Then the physicist fires a billiard ball at the go button from a considerable distance. Considered from n seconds before the billiard balls impact on the switch the total system (pond, edge jiggler, supporting apparatus and billiard ball) is isolated, and in this (temporarily) isolated system entropy decreases. (Though of course the entropy of the system including the scientist and all the mess she made in setting up this bizarre experiment overall increases.) The energy of the edge jiggling gets transferred into the rock and its motion out of the pond.

Are you now inclined to say that the rock leaping out of the pond caused the waves and precedent pond edge jiggling? According to the author you must, since causes are (by definition as it were) on the low end of high-entropy↔low-entropy paths, and the entropy of this isolated-during-the-jiggling-and-rock-moving system decreases during the edge-jiggling→rock throwing process. Indeed since the entropy level is the only thing we have to go on in determining time orientation then time itself is running backwards in this sub-system.

(p. 7) Precisely because causation does capture a time-oriented relation, it can only depend on the single source of time orientation that is compatible with physics: the thermodynamic arrow of time.

The only reason given for thinking that the direction of the arrow of time is determined by the entropy gradient (i.e. the future is by definition the temporal direction in which entropy increases) is that the author cannot think of an alternative that is compatible with the laws of physics.

But there are of course at least two alternatives: (1) Either there are time asymmetric physical laws that we haven't discovered yet. Laws that might, among other things, explain why entropy is always increasing (overall). OR (2) there might be something compatible with the laws of physics that we haven't yet considered.
