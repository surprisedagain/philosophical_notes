direction of time

short

What are the features which allow us to determine which direction in time is the future and which is the past?

Why is entropy higher in future? Is increasing entropy definitional (i.e. the future is whichever direction in time that entropy increases), due to an asymmetry in physical laws (and which temporal direction is the future can be determined with regard to that asymmetry) or something else?

the puzzle

Consider any one of the standard entropy exemplary systems. Boltzman's balls in microgravity is a good one, a stone falling into a pond is another, a solar system is also good.

The laws of physics determine the trajectories through the state space of this system. If the laws are determinate then these trajectories are continuous threads which contain one point in phase space at each moment, one point leading on to the next (and coming from the one before). If the laws of physics are probabilistic then each point throws a mist of probability concerning what point in the phase space it will occupy after a time Δt. For simplicity we will focus on the deterministic case, but bear in mind that objections that something or other doesn't apply in the probabilistic case may be legitimate.

Now on the one hand laws of physics are time symmetric, travel in either direction along these trajectories is permitted by the laws of physics. A trajectory from StateA to StateB is equally a trajectory from StateB to StateA.

But on the other hand the second law of thermodynamics says that entropy in this (total) system is monotonically increasing. That for all t1 and t2 if t2 is after t1 the entropy of the system at t2 is greater than or equal to the entropy of the system as t1. [ âˆ€t1, t2(Later(t2, t1) ⇒. E(S, t2) ≥ E(S, t1 ]

Now the puzzle is set - according to the laws of physics entropy decreasing paths are just as physically possible/likely as entropy increasing, but according to the second law we will only see entropy increasing paths.

solution 1

There are many fewer low entropy states than there are high entropy states.

This solution feels rather Bayesian. OK the number/likelihood of high entropy → low paths is exactly the same as that of low → high entropy paths but, the Bayesian says (as they always do) this is to ignore the base rate. The vast majority of paths are high → high entropy states.

Given that you have a path with one low entropy end, the odds are that the other end is high entropy. And this is perfectly compatible with there being equally many high→low as low→high paths. Whats unusual is that you have a path with a low entropy end at all.

But this seems to leave unexplained why the systems I see all come with low entropy earlier and high entropy later. And this problem can be considerably sharpened as follows:

Boltzmann brains

If I know that the system is within a ball in statespace (since all my observations/measurements have error bars) at this moment. And I know the laws of physics (i.e. the permitted statespace trajectories) so I also know that most of the trajectories passing through that ball of low entropy states go on (in future) to high entropy states. Thus I am justified in believing that the entropy of the system will increase in future.

But exactly the same line of reasoning leads me to believe that the entropy of the system will increase in past as well.

The puzzle, on this view, is not merely Why is entropy so low at the start of the universe? but How can I be justified in believing that entropy is low at the start of the universe?. i.e. Why does my justification for believing that entropy increases in future not also justify me in believing that it increases in past.

The conclusion is that you/(your brain) is the minimal divergence from a very high entropy state. While not a new rationale for skepticism it is a reductio ad absurdum of this explanation for (future) increasing entropy.
