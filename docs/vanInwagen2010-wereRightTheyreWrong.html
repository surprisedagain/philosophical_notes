main problem

weak exclusivism := It is "rational" to accept X AND X is incompatible with

other beliefs(/isms)

together with the "plausible principle" quoted below is claimed to entail
strong exclusivism := weak + it is not "rational" to accept

other incompatible beliefs(/isms)

the supporting "plausible claim" is something along the lines of the following:

(p.11.1) If it is rational for a person to accept a certain proposition, it cannot also be rational for that person (at the same time, in the same circumstances) to accept its denial.

which Inwagen ultimately fleshes out into the claim that:

(p.12.1) Consider any person as he is at a certain moment. That person has available to him, at that time, a body of evidence, his total evidence at that moment. Call it E. Of metaphysical necessity, E has the following property (of itself, regardless of who may have it) with respect to any proposition p: either (i) it would be rational for anyone to accept p on the basis of E, or (ii) it would be rational for anyone to accept the denial of p on the basis of E, or (iii) it would be not be rational for anyone to accept either p or the denial of p on the basis of E. This 'or' is exclusive. It cannot be that it would be rational to accept p on the basis of E and rational to accept the denial of p on the basis of E. Loosely speaking, a body of evidence cannot have the power to confer rationality on both a proposition and its denial. And, finally, it is rational for one to accept a proposition at a certain moment if and only if one's total evidence at that moment bears this impersonal "confers rationality upon" relation to that proposition.

This appears to force one of the following four "extraordinarily unappealing options" (where Ism is a controvertial philosophical doctrine one believes):

(p.14.1)
1: It is not after all rational for me to accept Ism.
2: It is not rational for any trained philosopher to accept Nism†a theory incompatible with Ism.
3: Some trained philosophers who accept Nism are in epistemic circumstances that are inferior to mine in the matter of deciding what to believe about Ism and Nism.
4: Some of the philosophers who accept Nism are less intelligent than me (or labor under some other relevant cognitive disadvantage; lack of philosophical talent, perhaps).

Inwagen is very unhappy about all these options.

As a penultimate solution he claims/conceeds/wails that

(p.15.1) therefore, the epistemological principles I have laid out, the principles that allowed us to deduce strong exclusivism from weak exclusivism, must be wrong.

(Thus he comes dangerously close to considering sum(P(t_given_e)_for_all_t)_must_be_over_1_for_some_e.card, but has already rejected it as ridiculous.)

As a final solution he considers access to private inchoate solutions

(p.17.1) . . . some sort of interior, incommunicable evidence

But in the end inwagen does not present a solution but rather as make the problem acute. (A brave and admirable admission of puzzlement.) And he is indeed correct that there is a problem, but it's not quite as bad or as insoluble as he supposes.

Further problems

Inwagen's claims may gain in credibility because, together with the quibbles described below, they appear to explain the following claim:

For each belief, and each collection of total evidence it is either justified or not to hold that belief. (And holding two mutually exclusive beliefs is never justified.) Insofar as one accepts a belief that is not justified, one is irrational.

While I disagree with nearly all the chief points in this claim, together they form something approximting common-sense. Hence Inwagen's claim gains credence by virtue of apparently offering an explanation of common-sense. It is further tedious to disentangle all the separate errors in this view, giving rise to the same difficulties as opposing a Gish Gallop.

a largely independent quibble

The paper suffers from the problem_with_treating_belief_as_binary. The focus throughout is on "rational to accept p" and never on "rational to accept p with degree of credence C"

This is manifest in the first section where inwagen argues: that since Ism is weakly exclusive then believers in Ism must (on pain of logical inconsistency no less ) hold that "The teachings of other religions are at least partly wrong."

Consider the following case. Suppose Aden is a "rational" believer in Ism. One who has good reason to believe Ism with a degree of confidence that is high but falls short of absolute certainty. It seems Aden may well recognise this fact, and hence be willing to assert that other religions are very likely to be wrong, but still unwilling to assert that the are simply wrong tout court. In hindsight for instance (having since had a revelation of the truth of some competing religion) Aden might realise that while the evidence available to him in the past had strongly supported Ism, and hence he had been rational to accept Ism's truth, in fact Nism had been true all along! But a reasonably undogmatic believer in Ism may accept that may be in such a position even at the time. That while their current belief in Ism is rational, it may be that Nism is (completely) true.

This only matters here in order to link I don't think this particularly matters the conversation can be reinterpreted mutatis mutandis in terms of degrees of confidence and degrees of justification. The claim now becomes that the credence which which evidence E warrants believing Ism and the credence E warrants in Nism (where the two are "logically inconsistent") must sum to less than 1 . In other words the negation of sum(P(t_given_e)_for_all_t)_must_be_over_1_for_some_e.card. It is hoped that just as inwagen did not argue for his supporting claims so much as "cast a very puzzled glance in the direction" of those who deny his supporting principles (quoted above).

another independent quibble

Bayesian's may insist further that we should not rest content with dispensing with the 2 place relationship "Evidence E warrants (or makes "rationally acceptable") belief in B" in favour of the place relationship "Evidence E warrants confidence C in belief B". They may wish to insist that "Evidence E warrants confidence C in belief B given priors P" is the most fundamental relationship, and that the other accounts of the relationships between evidence and what is justified by that evidence, insofar as they are true, are variously vague versions of this principle.
