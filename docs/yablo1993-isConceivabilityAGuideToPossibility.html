Let us change this to a more straightforward example - commanding. To command something is to do (or issue) something that has intentional content all right. On a more or less Gricean account of commanding perhaps issuing a command is just doing something that provides listeners(/observers) with evidence that one is in the mental state of commanding something. Or perhaps commands themselves are the objects with intentional content. But in either case commands don't have truth conditions. Someone who issues a command issues something that is not true or false but something that may be fulfilled or unfulfilled.

Just as the inward denial that De Gaulle liked cheese is not correct if DeGaulle liked cheese, so too the inward command that someone shut the door is not correct if someone shuts the door. One can both command that the door be shut and believe the door is not shut – and even believe that no-one will follow the order to shut the door – without contradicting oneself, which is to say without holding intentional states with contradictory truth conditions. Rather than having truth conditions, commands (even inward ones) have fulfillment or compliance conditions; conditions under which the orders is fulfilled or complied with, not conditions under which the order is true. Indicative sentences are true or false, imperative sentences are not.

Like commands, hopes do not have truth conditions either. They may be fulfilled or dashed. One response to the somewhat pedantic objection that hopes can come true is to claim that this is just a superficial way of speaking – a matter of equivocating between the different attitudes that one may have to the contents of beliefs and statements. This response is, perhaps, technically sufficient, it seems dubious. If we make free start ignoring this part of ordinary speech as a mere confusion what else may we dismiss. But in the spirit of ordinary language, it can be bolstered. We may note that at the moment that one comes to know that one's hopes are fulfilled they are not longer hopes, but now beliefs. One's attitude towards the same intentional contents has now changed, and what was once the fulfillment conditions of a hope has become the truth conditions of a belief. In the transmutation from hopes to beliefs, ones hopes do indeed come true. So too for wishing, dreading, yearning, approving, wanting and all the other intensional attitudes that we know and love. The do not have truth conditions but a variety of other fulfillment conditions.

These are the standard examples of alternative intentional attitudes. In all these cases the standard account is not that they involve different contents, but that they involve different attitudes towards the same contents. There are not special contents of desires that are different from the contents of beliefs and different again from the contents of . These intentional states are not distinguished by their contents
