preface

The model theory made this completely clear, though it should have been clear enough on the intuitive level. Waiving fussy considerations deriving from the fact that x need not have necessary existence, it was clear from (x) NN (x = x) and Leibnitz's law that identity is an 'internal' relation: (x) (y) (x = y ⊃ NN x = y). (What pairs (x, y) could be counterexamples? Not pairs of distinct objects, for then the antecedent is false; nor any pair of an object and itself, for then the consequent is true.) If 'a' and 'b' are rigid designators, it follows that 'a = b', if true, is a necessary truth. If 'a' and 'b' are not rigid designators, no such conclusion follows about the statement 'a = b' (though the objects designated by 'a' and 'b' will be necessarily identical).

The sense of Leibnitz's law that Kripke relies upon here is the less controversial (but less clearly said by Leibniz) indiscernability of identicals (see identity_of_indiscernables#Leibnizs law):

(x)(y)( x = y ⊃. F(x) ⊃ F(y) )
In this case the open formula in place of F(…) is: NN ( x = … ) producing:
(x)(y)( x = y ⊃. NN(x = x) ⊃ NN(x = y) )

from that and (z)NN(z = z) it may be inferred that

(x)(y)( x = y ⊃ NN(x = y) )

internal relations - all or none ??

At first glance this appears to be a beautiful explication of internal relations. It is at least prima facie appealing that a relation R is internal iff:

(x) (y) ( x R y ⊃ NN x R y )

But what gives this the sense of substance is the universal quantification of x and y. Does it really have to be the case that if a relationship is internal for one pair of objects then it is internal for all the pairs of objects for which it obtains? (And likewise for properties, if it is internal for one object/type of object must be be internal for all??). That is the claim made in that Kripke's suggested explication of internal property.

But consider the table in my kitchen and the table in my living room. I presume that it is not necessarily true that my kitchen table is longer than my living room table. If I dub my kitchen table Alia and my living room table Beethoven so that they are rigidly designated by these names then I presume (sadly I seem to lack the relevant modal intuitions) it is not necessarily the case that Alia is longer than Beethoven. Hence the relationship of being longer than is not an internal relationship by Kripke's definition.

But, to build upon an example Kripke presents in #lecture 1, consider the relationship between 2 metres and 1 metre (if you are uncomfortable reifying meters, me too, but it is Kripke's example). Surely it is necessarily the case that the length rigidly designated (according to Kripke) by 2 meters is longer than that designated by 1 meter. Does this make longer than an internal relationship for meter lengths even though it is not internal for every kind of object?

A natural suggestion is to can relativise internal relationships to types of object. Relation R is internal for objects of type T iff:

(x)(y) (T(x) and T(y) and x R y .⊃ NN x R y)

In this manner we may recognise Being longer than, as an internal relationship for lengths, but not for tables. But this won't seem to do:

weirdness with non-rigid designators

Lets focus on the last sentence:

If 'a' and 'b' are not rigid designators, no such conclusion follows about the statement 'a = b' (though the objects designated by 'a' and 'b' will be necessarily identical).

So lets say that Ali is the only red car on my street. Then Ali rigidly designates Ali and the definite description the red car on my street non-rigidly designates Ali. So too if Ali is the only Holden on my street then the definite description the Holden on my street also non-rigidly designates Ali.

Now it is not true that Necessarily the red car on my street is the Holden on my street but it is true that Necessarily, the object designated by 'the red car on my street' is identical to the object designated by 'the Holden on my street'. So within the scope of a NN replacing a name N with the object referred to be 'N' does not always preserve truth.

More generally NN P(the object referred to by 'Name') may be true and yet NN P(Name) be false. (We don't have to consider P as just a predicate here, but as any closed formula.) It is, I suppose, true that Necessarily the object designated by 'the red car on my street' was built in China. but false that Necessarily the red car on my street was built in China. but

We have not quite broken the principle that substituting any name for both instances of 'N' in 'N' designates N produces a truth. (A principle that seems seems every bit as non-negotiable as 'P' is true iff P.) But we do have a startling failure of substitutivity that comes close.

Naively one would have thought that quotation marks around a term (name or definite description) and the object refered to by …, would express kind of inverse operations. Just as quotation marks applied to sentences and is true. When one applies is true to a quoted sentence one gets another that is true just in case the quoted original is true. Indeed this is what (I thought) the disquotational theory of truth was all about. (See disquotational_truth)

So a sentence containing Necessarily can go from false to true by replacing the red car on my street with the object designated by 'the red car on my street'

This seems quite a high price to pay. For non rigid designators within the scope of necessity operators substitutivity fails.
