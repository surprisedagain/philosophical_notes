division between logic and metaphysics

It is well known that Kant's opinions about the prospect of further developments in logic was completely wrong

(Bviii) It is remarkable also that to the present day this logic has not been able to advance a single step, and is thus to all appearance a closed and completed body of doctrine.

The developments of Frege to bring logic up to the level of complexity required to capture recursively enumerable sets of sentences as theorems is still 100 years off.

But more importantly Kant offers to explain this completeness as due to logic being quite precisely delimited; its sole concern is to give an exhaustive exposition and a strict proof of the formal rules of all thought, whether it be a priori or empirical.

One wonders what Kant means by formal, surely not the modern view of rules specified purely in terms of the shapes and sequences of symbols without reliance unpon their meanings. Strict proof is also somewhat unclear, or at least foreign to modern logic - how might one prove an axiom of logic, or offer it any greater cfeedence than it already has? (inference to best explanation, or because of the fruitfulness of its consequences is urely not what Kant has in mind.)

metaphysical claims one must suppose, are by contrast those in which the validity of the reasoning, or the necessity of the claim, depends upon the content content (i.e. the actual predicate occupying a place) and not merely the form. i.e. logical laws are those that remain valid when a concept is consistently replaced with another, wheras such substitution can change a metaphysical necessity into a falsehood.

If this interpretation is correct then logical truths are neither analytic nor synthetic (unless the notion of containment is stretched enough to include containment of logical structure, but that seems implausible).

hint of argument against idealism

Kant repeatedly displays his awareness of the threat of self-contradiction in asserting that we positively know of the existence of objects about which we can know nothing.

Why posit noumena? We need a positive reasom. In the introduction the closest we come is

(Bxxvi) But our further contention must be duly borne in mind namely that though we cannot know these objects as things themselves we must yet be in position at least think them as things in themselves; otherwise we should be landed in the absurd conclusion they can be appearance without anything that appears.

This first hint, at least, is not particularly promising. It seems to hinge a definitional stop. If these things we experience are not appearances (and hence have some external/noumenal obejects that they are appearances of) then let us call them something else. The question then simply becomes what reason do we have for thinking that our ewxperienfes are appearances, and to this, as yet, Kant has ventured no answer

introduction

a priori

Nowhere here is Kant explicit about a priori knowledge being justified without experience. Instead we find talk of independent of experience or a posteriori knowledge as has its sources a posteriori or arises out of experience. The distinction between the origins, sources or arising out of is left unclear. If we accept, for the moment, the justified true belief analysis, one might have knowledge that a subject would not believe without experience or that would not be justified without experience. It is the kind of tiring prolixity that makes reading Kant such a slog.

This potential confusion is exacerbated by Kant's claim that Every alteration has its cause is a priori but not purely a priori on the grounds that alteration is a concept which can be derived only from experience. This derivation suggests that we cannot form the concept and hence, presumably, not form the judgement Every alteration has its cause without experience, regardless of whether or not we would have any justification for believing or disbelieving it. This distinction between _a priori_ and a posteriori concepts is thus far very dubious and we have no guide apart from this sole example on how to draw it.

Still the very fact that this is distinguished as an impure a priori proposition, goes further to suggest that it is justification that is in question. It appears that Kant/the translators can be only criticised for lack of clarity on this point and that on reflection it must be that a priori truths are those of which knowledge can be justified without experience. This is taken for granted, or as a definitional stipulation, in most modern ENglish speaking introductions to philosophy and it is merely of interest that it is put so unclearly in its most influential early exposition.

criterion

Necessity and certain universality as criterion of a priority.

Though counterexamples to these criteria have been constructed with the use of indexicals, they remain as the touchstone of my own enquiries, even though I ultimately give up on all three. Certainly, our knowledge of necessity could never be justified by experience. Is the underlying justification that there is no necessary connection between distinct existences? That Humean principle reminds me that the more fundamental problem is that experiences are caused and hence cannot provide evidence for necessity (and are evidence for possibility only in the sense that they are evidence for the actual and the actual is always possible).
