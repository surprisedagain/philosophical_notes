theses

modal isomorphism

representation modality Language represents a system(/the world) by virtue of an isomorphism between the permissible arrangements of symbols into sentences and the possible arrangements of the objects they represent.

This (purports to) explain how we know necessary truths about such external systems. We can be taught the grammatically permissible combinations of symbols by experience, and the modal structure of the represented systems MUST be the same since this is a pre-requisite of it being the thing (the type of thing) that those symbols represent.

The apparent dependency on normativity can be largely dispelled by simply specifying the relevant grammtical constructions explicitly. While it is normative rules that give rise to "grammaticality" in natural langauges, one can simply stipulate the set of grammatically correct sentences and thus establish the relevant space of gramatical possibilities.

There are at least two suspicions remaining however:

  1. Is there still a reliance upon an unresolved knowledge of modality?

At first, perhaps it seems that we surely know the possibilities of ur own minds better than the possibilities of some external objects/states of affairs. We can "directly" experience the possibilites of our own mind unconstriained by the limits of base external reality. How is it that we know what combinations of symbols are possible? But here it seems the combinations can be made purely of existing, if "abstract" entites and relationships. Consider quine's_account_of_logical_truth where the set of grammatically correct sentences and the set of logical truths is specified without any reliance upon modal notions at all.

  1. Is it really the case that "(6.43) . . . the_world_of_the_happy_man is a different one from that of the unhappy man"?

basis of logical space

Another aspect of W's approach that must be mentioned is the notion of finding a logical basis for the space of grammatically correct statements. This is directly analogous to the notion of a basis of a mathematical vector space. There the notion is that any vector in the space (mutatis mutandis any point in the space) can be reached/expressed/constructed as a combination*sum of multiples of the members of the basis.

sentential synonymy

That sentential synonymy is discernable is not a thesis of the Tractatus but rather an unquestioned assumption.

specific commentary

3.3*

The technique Wittgenstein uses in section 3.3* is what Carnap later calls quasi-analysis.

The reason for turning to Carnap is not to impose any of his theories of language, but because he lays out the technique in logically formal detail. In that sense, Carnap gives the clearest and most precise account of quasi-analysis. But be warned, in saying that Carnap is clear and precise I mean that his constructions are as explicit as formal logic, and not that his description of what he is doing is easy to understand. Carnap's initial description of quasi-analysis is cryptic:

Generally speaking . . . quasi analysis is to achieve the following: unanalyzable units of any kind, a pair list of which is presupposed, are to be manipulated with the constructional ascension forms of class and relation extension (i.e., with synthetic methods) in such a way that the result is a formal substitution for proper analysis (i.e., the analysis into constituents or properties), which cannot be carried out in this case.

Don't worry, an example Carnap gives in a moment will clarify this grammatical nightmare. But first let me attempt to rephrase his informal description in words that someone who wasn't already familiar with Carnap's project might understand:

Suppose we have a list of pairs of objects. In the cases we will be interested in these will be a list of pairs of objects that are like each other in some way. Each pair might have some property in common, but for whatever reason we are prevented from producing an analysis of these object's properties. All we know about these objects is which of pairs of them are on this list (i.e. we know the extension of the relationship similar-to).

On this basis of this single relationship of similar-to we can define terms that behave like ascriptions of properties that these objects have in common.

Let's try to get clearer on this by considering Carnap's first example:

Let there be altogether five different colors. Let us define the relation of "color kinship" in such a way that it is to hold for two things if these have at least one color in common. Let the things be individually designated, for example, by numbers. Now let us assume that we do not know of any of the things which colors it has. All we have is a pair list (i.e., we know only the extension of the relation of color kinship: we are told all pairs for which this relation holds, but we are not told which color these two things have in common). In other words, the extension of the relation of color kinship is completely given. Now, our task consists in inferring from these data the distribution of colors. We cannot proceed by choosing one of the things at random and determining all its color kin on the basis of the pair list, for it does not follow that all these are color akin to each other.

For instance, one object A might be both partly red (and hence similar[/related by colour kinship] to other objects that are at least partly red) and partly green (and hence similar[/related by colour kinship] to other green objects). But there may be two (or more) objects that are colour kin to A but not colour kin to each other (e.g. one object that is purely green and another that is purely red).

On the basis of this relation, provided it is symmetric (i.e. anything I am similar to is similar to me) and reflexive (each thing is similar to itself) we can determine how many different colours there are, and identify the extension of each colour (i.e. the classes of objects that are partly that colour), though of course we cannot identify the colours themselves. We might, for instance, determine that there are 5 colours and that one colour is shared by objects A, B and C and another colour and B, C, D and E all share another colour, but we cannot work out which colour is red or green etc., (Carnap has a problem of free-loaders. If each partly blue thing is also partly red, the extension of colour-kinship will not allow us to distinguish blue from red. Wittgenstein does not have this problem since he is concerned with the space of possible propositions.)

The two principles for assigning colours to objects (or rather objects to colour classes) are:

First of all, any two elements of a color class stand in the relation of color kinship to one another (because the members of the pair both have the color which determines the color class). Secondly, the color classes are the largest possible classes all of whose members are color-akin.

Carnap intends to use this technique to deliniate sensation types. Say, seeing a red square in the lower right quadrant of the visual field, or hearing the tone middle C. But he cant find unproblematic sensory elements from which to composes whole/gestalt sensations. In particular he is worried that the hearing of the chord composed of the notes C-E-G is not a sensation that is composed of hearing C, hearing E, and hearing G, but rather a sort of organic whole that taken by itself has nothing tri-partite about it. Rather than trying to identify the property Hearing-C(…), he instead simply identifies the set of sounds which are tone kin to each other without postulating some phenomena which is common to them all.

The trick hinges on quasi-identifying the property with the class of things that are related by the relevant similarity relation. Attributions of membership of the relevant class of objects related by colour/tone kinship (or whatever the relevant form of similarity is) can do the job of attributions of properties. e.g. instead of saying that A, B and C all have a colour in common, we can say that they are members of the largest class of objects related by colour similarity that includes A, B and C.

On the basis of the relation of colour similarity we can define a number of (one-place) predicates such as Colour1, Colour2, in terms of the (two-place) colour kinship relationship.

Instead of talking about the property of being red, or some other constituent of these objects that is supposed to be common between them, we can instead say that they are members of a colour class. (i.e. a maximal class of objects that are related to each other by the similar to relation).

We identify (in a loose sense of identity) the common property with the class of objects that are related to each other by similarity.

In short rather that saying P(a) we say [a is a member of {x: x is a member of a largest set of objects that all bear the similarity relationship to each other}].

Or as W puts it:

3.311 An expression presupposes the forms of all the propositions in which it can occur. It is the common characteristic mark of a class of propositions.

Here just as Carnap built talk of the common colours in terms of classes of objects that bear the relationship of colour kinship to each other, W is building talk of common expressions from classes of propositions that have some kind of similarity to each other.

3.312 It [the expression] is therefore presented by means of the general form of the propositions that it characterizes.

Lets see this construction in practice:

3.31 I call any part of a proposition that characterizes its sense an expression (or a symbol). (A proposition is itself an expression.) Everything essential to their sense that propositions can have in common with one another is an expression. An expression is the mark of a form and a content.

The first fundamental element of the construction is the relationship between propositions of having the same sense. For if a part characterises the sense of a containing proposition then changing that part will change the sense of the proposition - i.e. produce something that is either not a proposition at all, or is a proposition with a different sense, a proposition that it not synonymous with the original. Thus the first relationship from which we will build predicates true of expressions, symbols etc., is sentential synonymy. This plays the same role in Ws constructions that colour kinship or tone kinship played in Carnap's constructions.

The second fundamental relationship is substitution i.e. Sub(original proposition, element to be replaced, replacement, resulting proposition).In ordinary langauge, the result of replacing the element to be replaced in the original proposition with replacement is the resulting proposition.

So the sign cat is the class of things that can be substituted into the cat is on the mat without changing its meaning (i.e. which produces a proposition that is synonymous with the cat is on the mat). is-an-instance-of-the-sign-cat is a one place predicate that we are building out of substitution and synonymy relationships just as is-red was the one place predicate that Carnap built out of colour kinship relations. Now what we really want is not to specifically mention the particular proposition the cat is on the mat but rather to say that is-an-instance-of-the-sign-cat is true of just those things that can be substituted for any instance of the expression cat without changing their meaning (i.e. preserving sentential synonymy).

The expression cat is the class of propositions that contain the sign cat. There are complications for cat may be contained in various positions (i.e. directly or within some sub-sentential clause as in the cat is on the mat and the dog is in the dell). Although the construction is more complex it does give the pleasing result that

3.314 An expression has meaning only in a proposition. All variables can be construed as propositional variables. (Even variable names.)

Another and easier example to layout - the symbol (see 3.31 to confirm this is another term W uses for expression) and is the set of propositions that can be formed by filling out the schema [. . . and . . .]. And to say of a concrete proposition that it contains an instance of and is to say that it is a member of this set. (I have cheated simplified here by ignoring cases where and is not the primary or base connective.)

3.313 Thus an expression is presented by means of a variable whose values are the propositions that contain the expression. (In the limiting case the variable becomes a constant, the expression becomes a proposition.) I call such a variable a ‘propositional variable’. [see point 2 below]

Having gone over the relevant technique of logical (well set-theoretical really) construction at some length (though not in formal detail) let me make my claims clear.

  1. W has these techniques of construction in mind in sections 3.3*. I believe that W, while a genius of the first order and an extraordinary philosopher, is not a particularly strong logician. Hence I believe that he has seen this technique of construction in use (perhaps by Russell or Whitehead, but perhaps by others seeking [as we now would put it] to found mathematics in set theory) and sees that it can be applied to the problems he is interested in. W's techniques are not like or inspired by these forms of construction they are precisely what he has in mind, though he omits many details and merely says enough to assure himself (and his idealised readers) that this construction can be carried through.
  2. A difference between constructed/quasi-analysed predicates and normal/naive predicates is that this construction relies upon the existence of sets of objects. You paraphrase x is P as x is a member of a specified maximal set of objects bearing a similarity relationship to each other. Russell and friends were particularly callow with regard to this extra commitment - treating a set as equivalent to a propositional function - i.e. the set of red things is (in some sense) the incomplete symbol Red(. . .). Wittgenstein follows suit, and is equally unconcerned over the existence of sets, and equally uninterested in distinguishing propositional schema from the set of propositions that instantiate that schema.
  3. This is what logic is good for. You may have noticed me criticising attempts to paraphrase philosophical arguments in terms of formal logic. The criticism being that if the resulting version of the argument is not logically valid, the proponent of the original argument can simply maintain that the paraphrase is inadequate and very little has been achieved. If really pressed they can claim that the logic in question is not adequate to the form of argument (this is Brentano's move, commonly used to reject extensionalist logics). By contrast on my view logic is a tool of construction. That you can build talk of properties from relations of (symmetrical, reflexive) similarity (and some set theory) is a genuine achievement worth noting.
  4. Ws view of analysis here is akin to Russells view in Principia Mathematica. Both W and R take themselves not to be merely showing one way (among many others) of constructing the desired pattenr of inferences, but to be revealing a structure already present in the objects they are analysing. Although I have in a broadly modern/(Carnapian maybe) spirit described this as a construction, you can look at this as an epistemic relationship. If you can determine the synonymy relationships between sentences then you get epistemic access to the rest of the machinery of signs, symbols and the space of possible propositions. This is the immediate purpose of the technique.

Finally, the place of this in W's larger project. This is part of a three step process:

1 Identify the expressions of the langauge and how they may be arranged so as to form propositions. The fundamental semantic relationship upon which this step relies is sentential-synonymy (together with the non-sematic relationship of substitution).

2 Describe the resulting expressions/range of propositions (a recursive definition will be required) in purely natural/non-semantic terms. This is when you must not rely upon semantic properties to deliniate the class of propositions:

3.317 To stipulate values for a propositional variable is to give the propositions whose common characteristic the variable is. The stipulation is a description of those propositions. The stipulation will therefore be concerned only with symbols, not with their meaning. And the only thing essential to the stipulation is that it is merely a description of symbols and states nothing about what is signified. [Italics in original]
How the description of the propositions is produced is not essential.
3.33 In logical syntax the meaning of a sign should never play a rôle. It must be possible to establish logical syntax without mentioning the meaning of a sign: only the description of expressions may be presupposed.

3 You have now deliniated the space of possible propositions in purely natural/epistemically accessible terms and have thus both (a) drawn(/shown) the limits of what can be thought and (b) given an account of our knowledge of necessary (i.e. analytic) truths that is compatible with naturalism/empiricism.

Finally, note that this whole edifice is built upon the relationship of sentential synonymy which is taken as fundamental. If that relationship is epistemically inaccessible then the edifice collapses. This is one way of appreciating why Quine's attack is so far-reaching.

If the world is the totality of facts not things, then a language is a totality of propositions not symbols. It is upon the relationship of synonymy between sentences that the identification of symbols, hence the space of possible propositions, hence the account of representation offered in the Tractatus relies.
