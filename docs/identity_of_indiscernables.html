Leibnizs law

According to Tarski, Leibniz's Law is the conjunction of both the identity of indiscernibles and the indiscernability of identicals:

Among the logical laws concerning the concept of identity the most fundamental is the following: x = y if, and only if, x has every property which y has, and y has every property which x has. This law was first stated by Leibniz (although in somewhat different terms) and hence may be called Leibniz' Law. (Introduction to Logic, 1941).

According to Feldman (Leibniz and "Leibniz' Law", 1970) Leibniz states the principle of the identity of indiscernibles quite clearly in his Discourse on Metaphysics:

… it is not true that two substances may be exactly alike and differ only numerically, solo numero …

One might naively have thought that the indiscernability of identicals was so uncontroversial as to go without saying? This is, as one might expect, the topic of scholarly debate. Some, such as Tarski above, interpreting Leibniz as asserting the bi-conditional, while others interpret him as asserting only the identity of indiscernibles.

principle of substitution

Leibniz also states something akin to a principle of substitution:

Eadem sunt quorum unum in alterius locum substitui pote salva veritate
(Gerhardt, Die Philosophischen Schriften von G. W. Leibniz Vol. VII)

which has been translated as:

Things are the same (or identical), one of which can be substituted in place of the other with preservation of truth

There is in this, no recognition of what Quine would call referentially opaque (or non-extensional) contexts such as are created by modal operators and (are generally thought to be created by) intentional idioms (believes, hopes, etc.,). Furthermore it is not things themselves that are substituted salva veritate*Quine explicitly attributes this phrase to Leibniz in S3 of Two Dogmas. for each other but their names. On this interpretation (plausibly attributed to Quine) one must take Leibniz as careless of the distinction between use and mention - between Cicero and Cicero.

Feldman advances the theory that this is an identity criteria for concepts, such as triangular and trilateral, quadrangle and quadrilateral. In this sense it makes sense to say that two concepts are identical if their substitution for each other (in the relevant judgement or whatever sentence-like entity it is that concepts can compose) always leaves the truth of the judgement unchanged.

My suspicion, is that Leibniz is simply careless of the distinction. Treating Cicero, Cicero and concepts all as objects. The confusion made especially attractive that each object has a proper name (characteristica universalis ???? this is the name of the language)

Is an object simply its object concept. The echo of this monad in that monad over there?

identification of indiscernibles

Quine inverts this with his identification of indiscernibles:

Suppose a discourse about person stages, and suppose that whatever is said about any person stage, in this particular discourse, applies equally to all person stages which make the same amount of money. Our discourse is simplified, then, by shifting its subject matter from person stages to income groups. Distinctions immaterial to the discourse at hand are thus ex- truded from the subject matter.
In general we might propound this maxim of the identification of indiscernibles: Objects indistinguishable from one another within the terms of a given discourse should be construed as identical for that discourse. More accurately: the references to the original objects should be reconstrued for purppses of the discourse as referring to other and fewer objects, in such a way that indistinguishable originals give way each to the same new object.
(quine1961-fromALogicalPointOfView.pdf)

This reflects Quine's idea that for any discourse/language/theory/formal system with a finite stock of predicates and the standard logical apparatus we can define identity as follows:

x = y .def=. A(x) ↔ A(y) and B(x) ↔ B(y) …
(source???? methods of logic? selected logic papers?)

This is deeply appealing. It is we that divide up the world into objects. Though there is no way to describe the world without doing so. That division is intimately connected with the predicates we take to be true of those objects. Of course, how else could it be?
