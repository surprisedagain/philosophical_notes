So too, for the verb to believe without justification. If I know my belief to be merely caused I thence reject that belief.

example of mere cause

Consider the following example: Why do you believe there is a coin in the magicians pocket?
Because the magician has a wire loop that makes a circular outline in the fabric, and the magician performed a retention vanish which made it look like they placed a coin in their pocket (when they really retained it in their hand) and the metal detector was gimmicked to go off when the assistant presses a secret button and not just in the presence of metals and the assistant did press the button when they passed the detector over the pocket etc., etc., Together these caused me to believe the magician placed a coin in their pocket.

These are things which regularly do cause people to believe there is a coin in the magicians pocket (regularly enough for magicians to make a living from their craft) and it is entirely plausible (in the third person as it were) that such a sequence of events would cause someone to believe there is a coin in the magicians.

However, when I recognise that my belief has been caused in this way then I abandon my belief that there is a coin in the magician's pocket. If I recognise that the cause of my believe is not also a good reason for my belief - does not justify me in holding that belief - then the moment I recognise that my belief has been caused by something that does not justify it I reject the belief.

potential counter-examples

double causation

I recognise my belief was caused by a deception/illusion/error of some kind. But before discovering this, my belief is justified in some other way.

Vary the example of the coin in the magicians pocket: Suppose the magician performs a sleight that makes it appear that they have put a coin in their pocket. As before, this, together with other supporting evidence, the circular outline in the fabric, the beep of the metal detector when it passes over the pocket etc., causes me to believe that there is a coin in the magicians pocket.

Suppose further that I have bribed the assistant to signal when the magician really places a coin in their pocket (a slightly delayed signal). The assistant does make this signal and my belief that there is a coin in the magician's pocket is confirmed.

However, I go on to see the magician apparently placing other coins in their pocket and I am certain that some of these placements are faked, but I can't tell which. I become aware that the magician's moves are much more convincing than I had first thought. The original causes of my belief (the way the magician's hands moved or my observation thereof) did not in fact justify my belief that the magician placed a coin in their pocket. (They could have quite well made identical movements without leaving the coin in the pocket.) Before the assistant's delayed signal my belief was unjustified (among other things it was neither safe nor sensitive). The observations I made can be produced by the magician, more or less at will, without them placing a coin in their pocket and hence caused but did not justify my belief.

In answer to the question Why do you believe the magician placed a coin(/the first coin) in their pocket?, I might now answer Because I paid the assistant a considerable sum to signal me (somewhat after the event) when the magician does in fact place a coin in their pocket and I have seen them make that signal. But this is not in fact what caused my belief. What caused my belief in the first place was the way the magicians hands moved, the circular outline in the fabric of their pocket and so on.

We can distinguish two questions concerning the causation of my belief: Why do you now believe that … vs Why did you form the belief that …. The answer to the first question is I saw the attendant's signal. the answer to the latter I saw the magicians hands come out of his pocket and they looked completely empty …

Note also that the example works whether or not the assistants signal's are correct. The assistant may be telling me the truth or acting to protect his performing partner. So long as my trust in the assistant is justified.

In any case when i realise that my belief was merely caused by my observations (i.e. that my belief was [originally] caused, but not justified, by my observations of the movements of the magicians hands etc.,) I may not immediately also reject my belief, because in the meantime some other evidence (the assistant's signal) has come along that both caused and justified my continued belief that there is a coin in the magicians pocket. This is thus a counter-example to the claim that when I realise my belief was only caused but not justified I reject it.

mental disorder or magic spell

A more extreme example of mental disorder might produce a counter example. Suppose I am somehow compelled to believe something, for instance, that I will die I step outside my house. Suppose that I have mental disorder which compels me to hold this belief. I might be aware of this, I have read the medical reports, seen the scan of the very particular brain lesion, understand that my belief is merely caused by this brain damage, and yet still be unable to shake it.
