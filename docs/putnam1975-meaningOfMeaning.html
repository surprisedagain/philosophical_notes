short

A supposed a posteriori necessary truth, inferred from a combination of a priori analytic and a posteriori synthetic truths. Based upon an analysis of natural kind terms. Like Kripke's a posteriori necessary truths these hinge upon taking natural kind terms as involving quasi-indexical rigid designators.

water is H2O

The argument:

Water is H2O is inferred from a combination of necessary truths known a priori and contingent truths discovered by empirical investigation. The necessary truth is:

"water" is stuff that bears a certain similarity relation to the water around here. Water at another time or in another place or even in another possible world has to bear the relation sameL*i.e. same_liquid to our "water" in order to be water.

This principle was an analysis of the term water. Putnam claimed, quite convincingly, that water was a natural kind term, and the predominant sense of natural kind words was one in which they were true of whatever things share the nature that things satisfying the description normally possess.. Sharing the nature was a matter of having the same hidden structure*To sum up: if there is a hidden structure, then generally it determines what it is to be a member of the natural kind, not only in the actual world, but in all possible worlds. (_ibid.,) And in the case of . is one in which being a member of natural kind is a matter of having the same Which, in the case of liquids such as water, was a matter of having the same chemical structure. In other words, the statement All water bears the relationship sameL to the water around here. is true by virtue of the meanings of the terms it contains. (Most particularly, though of course not solely, the meaning of the term water.)
